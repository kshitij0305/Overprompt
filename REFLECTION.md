# Reflection

## Initial Expectations

When I first started the assignment, I assumed the hardest part would be the technical implementation.

I initially approached the project thinking mostly about:
- frontend structure
- backend complexity
- framework setup
- deployment

Very quickly, I realized the assignment was actually testing something much broader:
- product thinking
- prioritization
- reasoning quality
- ability to build a believable MVP quickly

That realization completely changed how I approached the project.

---

# Learning Through Building

This was my first serious project using Next.js.

At the beginning, even small framework errors felt intimidating. I spent time struggling with:
- App Router structure
- layout configuration
- component organization
- state handling

What helped the most was building incrementally instead of trying to understand the entire framework at once.

As the project evolved, I became much more comfortable:
- debugging errors
- structuring components
- managing state
- reasoning about frontend architecture

One thing I learned during this process is that frameworks become much less overwhelming when they are tied to a real product goal instead of isolated tutorials.

---

# Product Thinking Changed The Most

The biggest shift during development was how I thought about the recommendation engine.

Initially, my recommendations were unintentionally biased toward certain platforms because it was easier to write simplistic logic quickly.

The product started feeling unrealistic almost immediately.

I realized that if every recommendation aggressively pushed users toward one platform, the audit engine would lose credibility.

That forced me to rethink the entire product.

Instead of asking:
“Which AI tool is best?”

I started asking:
“Is this team actually getting enough value from what they are paying for?”

That shift changed the recommendation logic completely.

The product became less about AI opinions and more about:
- pricing efficiency
- workflow alignment
- plan utilization
- subscription overlap

Once I reframed the problem that way, the recommendations started feeling much more believable.

---

# The Importance of Trust

One of the strongest realizations during development was that AI-related products depend heavily on trust.

Users are unlikely to trust recommendations that:
- feel overly opinionated
- sound fake-AI-generated
- aggressively favor specific vendors
- cannot explain their reasoning

Because of this, I intentionally kept the audit engine deterministic and explainable instead of pretending the app used advanced AI reasoning.

Ironically, avoiding fake “AI magic” made the product feel more realistic.

---

# Balancing Polish vs Progress

Another challenge was resisting the urge to endlessly polish the UI.

It was very easy to spend large amounts of time:
- tweaking spacing
- adjusting typography
- redesigning layouts

while avoiding harder product decisions.

At one point I realized the landing page looked increasingly polished, but the product itself still lacked meaningful functionality.

After that, I tried to focus more on:
- core product loops
- recommendation quality
- user interaction
- believable outputs

instead of purely visual improvements.

That tradeoff helped progress move much faster.

---

# Research Was More Important Than Expected

Initially, I underestimated how important pricing research would be.

As I started researching ChatGPT, Claude, Cursor, and other platforms, I realized the project was actually closer to:
a SaaS optimization product

than an AI comparison tool.

Understanding:
- pricing tiers
- collaboration features
- workflow positioning
- premium upsells

became essential for creating believable recommendation logic.

The research phase also helped me understand how quickly AI subscription complexity is growing for individuals and small teams.

---

# MVP Discipline

One thing I learned from this project is that building a believable MVP is mostly about making good tradeoffs.

There were many features I considered adding:
- authentication
- databases
- analytics dashboards
- persistent reports
- multi-tool workflows

But I realized that trying to build everything would likely weaken the core product.

Instead, I focused on:
- a clean audit flow
- understandable recommendations
- lightweight UX
- explainable logic

That made the project feel more coherent overall.

---

# What I Would Improve Next

If I continued developing the project, the next improvements would likely include:
- multi-tool audit support
- persistent audit history
- real usage analytics
- recommendation confidence scoring
- API spend tracking
- exportable reports

I would also spend more time validating assumptions through real user interviews and testing actual subscription behavior.

---

# Final Thoughts

This project changed how I think about building software.

At the start, I was focused mostly on:
“Can I technically build this?”

By the end, I was thinking more about:
- whether users would trust it
- whether recommendations felt believable
- whether the product solved a real operational problem
- whether the experience felt coherent

The most valuable lesson from this assignment was realizing that a good product is not necessarily the most technically complicated one.

Often, the hardest part is creating something simple enough to understand, but believable enough that users take it seriously.
